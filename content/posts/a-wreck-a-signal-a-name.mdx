The wreck had no registry left.

Whatever it had been before the guns found it, it was now ribs and cold storage floating three hundred kilometers above an Ezzara belt. I nearly ignored it. That is the part I keep replaying. I nearly let it remain debris because the scanner called it debris.

Then the signal repeated.

Thirty-one seconds apart. Narrow band. Low power. One word, or what I thought was one word, trapped under static and capacitor bleed.

I pulled the fragment into the bay and isolated the transmitter. The first pass gave me nonsense. The second gave me a consonant chain I recognized from an old Starkmanir naming pattern. The third pass gave me enough to stop breathing for a moment.

Not a distress call.

A name.

I will not write it here until the archive verifies lineage and consent. Names are not salvage. They are not trophies. They are doors, and not every door should be opened by the first capsuleer with a working analyzer.

Still, I sat with it for twenty minutes before docking.

The signal continued every thirty-one seconds. Patient. Ruined. Insistent.

There are people who think the dead are quiet because they have nothing left to say. They have never listened properly to a wreck.
