The Ezzara pocket was hiding behind interference that looked natural until it repeated itself.

Three pulses. Pause. Three pulses. Pause. Like a hand knocking from inside a sealed room.

The first container broke open under a standard analyzer cycle and gave me a devotional shard, old enough that the language bent around my tongue. Not fully Starkmanir, not fully anything the Republic archivists will enjoy categorizing. The second held a household ledger: ration marks, route names, birth counts, three references to a grandmother whose name was overwritten by a later hand.

I hated that hand immediately.

The third object was a blackened strip of alloy with markings too shallow for confidence. I ran three scans. The computer suggested manufacturing codes, then ritual notation, then damage artifacts. Machines dislike admitting confusion. I logged all three outputs and sealed the fragment separately.

There is a temptation in recovery work to make every find beautiful. That is a lie. Most of what we recover is damaged, compromised, misfiled, or made ugly by the hands that held it captive. Beauty is not the standard. Continuity is.

I transmitted copies to the archive node and kept the originals aboard until I could verify the chain of custody.

On the return gate, a hostile scout landed seventy kilometers off and watched me align. We stared at each other through instruments and bad history.

Neither of us fired.

Maybe they saw an easy frigate. Maybe they saw a cargo hold full of ghosts and decided they had enough of their own.
